# General Rules

## Dice System

The game primarily uses a d10-based system for most checks. It focuses on rolling one or two ten-sided dice to determine success or failure.

## Required Materials

### Dice

- 1d4: tetrahedral die, used for low-range outcomes or minor damage.
- 1d6: classic cubic die, used for moderate outcomes.
- 1d8: octahedral die, used for intermediate outcomes and damage.
- 1d10: decahedral die, used for most game checks.
- 1d12: dodecahedral die, used for high outcomes and damage.
- 1d20: icosahedral die, mainly used for checks related to creature health.

### Character Sheets

Documents used to track each character's stats, skills, equipment, and progression.

### Tokens

Markers used to represent characters, creatures, and other important elements on a board or map.

## Specific-over-General Rule

If there is a conflict between a specific rule and a general rule, the specific rule takes precedence.

### Example

If an ability defines duration as Level / 2 (rounded down), that specification overrides the general round-up rule.

## Narrative Flexibility Rule

The Narrator may adapt, modify, or omit rules to strengthen narrative flow and keep the table experience smooth.

These adjustments must be:

- fair
- consistent
- transparent

The goal is to prioritize coherence, collaboration, and player enjoyment over rigid rules enforcement.

## Rounding Rule

Whenever a calculation produces fractions or decimals, round up to the nearest whole number, unless a specific rule says otherwise.

This applies to:

- damage
- durations
- ratings
- other relevant numeric values

## Strongest Condition Rule

When a creature receives multiple bonuses or penalties of the same type, only the most significant one applies.

### Application

If an effect is already active and a new effect of the same type is applied, compare magnitude and impact. The stronger one prevails.

### Overwriting Existing Effects

If the new condition is stronger, or equally strong but longer in duration, it replaces the existing condition and resets duration from the new application.

### Example

Thorgar receives a penalty that halves movement speed for 3 turns. Later he receives another same-type movement penalty (-2 movement speed) for 6 turns. The strongest condition remains active, and duration becomes 6 turns.

## Tool Handling Rule

Crafting, repairing, refining, and specialization activities require task-specific tools.

### Alternative Tools

If a character lacks the exact tools, similar tools may be used at higher difficulty.

The Narrator evaluates whether the substitution is viable and narratively coherent.

## Adventure Cycle

Adventure Cycles structure play without excessive micromanagement. Each phase clarifies what is happening and which rules are active.

### Exploration Phase

In this phase, players explore locations, uncover mysteries, seek resources, and interact with the world.

Common behaviors:

- environment assessment
- maintenance activities
- rest and recovery
- planning and strategy

### Conflict Phase

This phase resolves high-risk encounters, combat, and tactical obstacles.

#### Terms

- **Creature**: any entity participating in play (PC, NPC, or monster).
- **Player Character (PC)**: protagonist controlled by a player.
- **Non-Player Character (NPC)**: world figure controlled by the Narrator.
- **Monster**: NPC subtype focused on instinctive or unnatural behavior.

#### Conflict Flow

1. The Narrator sets the conflict conditions.
2. Place each creature token on the initiative track.
3. The leftmost creature on the track acts.
4. The active creature reviews status, picks intent, and chooses an action.
5. The creature declares intent.
6. Possible reactions are checked.
7. Interruptions or responses are resolved.
8. The main action is resolved.
9. Move the token on the initiative track.
10. After all creatures activate, repeat from step 3 until the conflict ends.

### Interlude Phase

Extended period where characters handle important activities outside detailed scene-by-scene play.

Common activities:

- gear creation and improvement
- recovery and rest
- resource management
- planning and strategy
- personal development and relationships
