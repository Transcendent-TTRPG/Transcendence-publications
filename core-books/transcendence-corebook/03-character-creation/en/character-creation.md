# Character Creation

Character creation is the process through which you define who your character is, where they come from, and what capabilities they have developed before the adventure begins. In this system, a character is not built only from numbers, but from the relationship between their nature, their experience, and the way both have shaped their body, mind, and behavior.

Every character emerges from the combination of three core elements: species, which defines their biological and cultural foundation; background, which represents the life they lived before becoming an adventurer; and specializations, which express the specific areas where they developed practice, craft, or discipline. From these elements come their characteristics, derived attributes, and overall way of confronting the world.

Creating a character means moving through a sequence of steps in order, each building on the previous one. First, you define the general concept. Then you establish the initial base, choose species and background, select starting specializations, and apply the effects those choices have on characteristics. Finally, you calculate derived attributes, choose personality traits, and record additional attributes granted by species.

This process does not only prepare a sheet; it gives shape to a coherent playable identity. A character is not only someone with certain values, but a creature with heritage, habits, strengths, limitations, and a prior history that explains why they can do what they do.

## Character Concept

Before choosing values, traits, or specializations, the first step is to define your character concept. A concept is a brief but clear idea of who your character is as an individual. It does not need to be a full backstory, but it should answer the central question: what kind of person or creature is this character within the world?

The concept guides every later decision. It helps you choose a species that makes sense, a background aligned with your past, specializations consistent with your experience, and personality traits that reflect how you think and act. The clearer the concept, the easier it is to build a solid and consistent character.

A good concept usually includes three elements: a general identity, a motivation, and a way of relating to the world. General identity defines what kind of figure the character represents. Motivation expresses what drives them to act. Their relation to the world helps explain how they face danger, uncertainty, others, and their own internal conflicts.

The concept should not be limited to a mechanical role. It is not enough to think, "I want to make a strong character" or "I want to make a stealthy one." It is better to imagine the person behind those capabilities. For example, a brutal warrior raised for war does not feel the same as a patient hunter who learned to survive in solitude, even if both end up sharing some physical strengths. In the same way, two characters with the same background can feel entirely different if their motivations, temperament, and worldview are not the same.

It is also important for the concept to fit the tone of the campaign and the kind of story the group wants to tell. A character can be strange, complex, or contradictory, but should still feel part of the same world as everyone else. The ideal concept explains not only who the character is, but why it makes sense for them to be part of the adventure.

If you want, summarize your concept in a single sentence before continuing. That sentence does not replace your character's history, but it works as a guide through the whole creation process.

## Base Characteristics

Base characteristics represent the character's fundamental pillars. They express physical, mental, and social aptitudes before those aptitudes translate into concrete actions during play. They are not only innate talent: they are also the structure on which capabilities grow through species, background, and experience.

When creating a character, all characteristics begin at 0. This initial value does not represent incompetence, but a neutral baseline on top of which modifiers granted by species and later by Synapsis are applied.

Characteristics are divided into three groups: Physical, Mental, and Social.

### Physical Characteristics

#### Strength

Strength represents the character's physical power and their ability to apply force effectively to the environment. It influences actions like lifting weight, breaking obstacles, grappling, pushing, or striking with force. A character with high Strength can dominate through raw vigor and make better use of weapons or maneuvers that depend on physical power.

#### Agility

Agility reflects coordination, reaction speed, and bodily precision. It governs balance, movement, execution of delicate physical maneuvers, and adaptation to the motion of combat or environment. A character with high Agility moves with ease, dodges more effectively, and performs physical actions with greater control.

#### Tenacity

Tenacity indicates physical endurance, the character's ability to withstand fatigue, pain, and prolonged strain. It also reflects recovery capacity against wear and physical adversity. A character with high Tenacity can remain standing longer, endure harsh conditions better, and sustain higher levels of effort.

### Mental Characteristics

#### Intellect

Intellect measures the character's reasoning, memory, and formal understanding. It covers logical analysis, academic knowledge, and ease in understanding complex concepts or recalling important information. A character with high Intellect excels at study, research, interpretation, and structured problem-solving.

#### Cunning

Cunning represents mental sharpness, practical creativity, and the ability to find effective solutions in changing situations. It depends less on accumulated knowledge and more on improvisation, deception, reading intentions, or finding alternative paths when circumstances grow difficult. A character with high Cunning is usually adaptable, resourceful, and hard to surprise.

#### Wisdom

Wisdom represents perception, intuition, and the ability to interpret the world and its inhabitants correctly. It reflects applied experience, judgment, and sensitivity to nuance in environments, behavior, or complex situations. A character with high Wisdom often notices what others miss and makes decisions with clearer judgment.

### Social Characteristics

#### Composure

Composure reflects self-control, emotional stability, and the ability to remain calm under pressure. It is the basis of inner discipline when dealing with social tension, psychological threats, or delicate situations. A character with high Composure resists manipulation better, stays calm more easily, and makes firmer decisions when others break down.

#### Aura

Aura is the natural and involuntary impression the character projects onto others. It is not a trained skill, but the imprint of their nature: a mixture of instinct, temperament, and essential presence that can be perceived even before speaking or acting. Aura defines how the character's existence feels to those around them, and can inspire fascination, unease, respect, or rejection.

#### Presence

Presence represents the character's active ability to project themselves into the environment and affect the behavior, perception, or mood of others. Unlike Aura, which is passive and involuntary, Presence implies intention, force of personality, and character. A character with high Presence can impose, inspire, intimidate, persuade, or draw attention more easily.

## Initial Value of Characteristics

During character creation, all base characteristics start at 0.
From there, value changes in two stages:

- Species provides the first increases, representing innate baseline.
- Synapsis adds new increases as specializations gain competency ranks.

In this way, final characteristics are the result of combining what the character is by nature and what they have developed through experience.

## Derived Attributes

Some values are not base characteristics, but derived attributes calculated from several of them. These include, for example, Preparation and Resilience. They are calculated later, once species, background, and Synapsis have already modified characteristics.

## Choose Species

Species defines the character's biological and cultural baseline. It does not only determine appearance or origin, but also what kind of body they inhabit, what inherited adaptations they carry, how they move through the world, and what distinctive traits belong to their nature from the beginning.

In narrative terms, species places the character in the world. It indicates which people they belong to, what place their culture holds in the continent's history, and what traditions, habits, beliefs, or tensions they may carry from origin. Choosing species is not only choosing bonuses; it is deciding what kind of being you are before background, discipline, and experience finish shaping you.

In mechanical terms, species establishes the character's first permanent traits. Each species offers its own combination of attributes and capabilities. When choosing one, record every element shown in its species profile.

Each species includes, at minimum, the following elements:

### Longevity

Indicates the species' typical lifespan. It does not directly alter core character rules, but helps understand relation to time, memory, tradition, and change.

### Size

Defines size category, and therefore physical presence, occupied space, and interaction with environment.

### Height and Weight

Describe the species' usual physical range. These values support narrative reference and visualization.

### Languages

Each species has its own language and, in most cases, access to the common tongue. This defines communication baseline at game start.

### Speed

Determines how much distance the character can cover during a movement action and reflects body and locomotion adaptations.

### Characteristic Bonuses

Each species grants three initial increases to specific characteristics. These increases represent the character's innate baseline and are the first source of characteristic growth before Synapsis from specializations.

### Other Equivalent Starting Values

Some species may grant additional starting values tied to natural capacities, such as specific competencies, specialized senses, or innate traits outside the base characteristics.

### Heritage

Heritage represents a vulnerability, limitation, or ancestral mark that accompanies the species. It is not an accidental weakness, but a consequence of evolutionary history, biology, or ties to certain world forces.

### Legacy

Legacy represents innate species advantages: natural talents, resistances, sensory adaptations, or special capabilities shared by members to varying degrees.

### Unarmed Attacks or Natural Weapons

Many species possess innate forms of combat, such as fangs, claws, horns, tails, pincers, or stingers. These natural weapons are not aesthetic details: they are part of species martial identity and can define important parts of playstyle.

### Variants

Some species have internal variants. A variant does not replace the species, but expresses a distinct adaptation within the same lineage. Variants usually modify specific traits, especially natural weapons or certain focused capabilities.

## What Happens When You Choose a Species

When you choose species, your character immediately gains their natural baseline. This includes biological and cultural identity, languages, size, speed, initial characteristic bonuses, and any additional racial trait listed in the profile.

At this step, what the character learned or refined is still not defined. That comes later through background and starting specializations. Species expresses what the character is by nature; background and Synapsis express what they have come to develop.

## Species and Playstyle

Each species does not only modify numbers; it also suggests a way to inhabit the world. Some stand out through mobility, others through resilience, others through perception, presence, or unique adaptations. Species with strong natural weapons or marked variants can open combat styles that do not depend on crafted weapons.

For this reason, when choosing species, consider both narrative concept and the kind of play experience you want. Species should feel coherent with both: character identity and expression at the table.

### Important

Complete species descriptions, including qualities, legacy, heritage, variants, and natural weapons, are in the Species chapter. During character creation you only need to choose one and record every attribute indicated in that profile.

## Choose Background

Background represents the character's life before adventuring. It defines what experiences shaped them, in what environment they learned to survive, and in which areas they had already developed practice, discipline, or knowledge before story start. While species expresses what the character is by nature, background expresses what they learned and cultivated through prior life.

Choosing a background is not only choosing an interesting history, but determining the first concrete form of the character's experience. Through background you define what specialization fields come most naturally, what kind of training they received, and what areas progress faster once the adventure begins. In that sense, background is the bridge between narrative concept and first mechanical capabilities.

Each background grants two primary benefits: a major affinity and a set of starting specializations. Major affinity indicates the specialization category where the character progresses faster. When a specialization belongs to that affinity, it levels with 5 progress; others require 10. This represents a stronger prior foundation in that type of training.

In addition, each background allows three starting specializations under its own rules. Each starts at Level 1, equivalent to Rank 1, and the same option cannot be chosen twice. Because those specializations already start with rank, they immediately generate first Synapsis effects, increasing associated characteristics.

All competencies not coming from background begin at Level 0, so the character is considered Untrained in them at start. This does not mean they can never develop them, only that they are not part of initial baseline. They can improve them during play under normal progression rules.

Narratively, background answers a simple but fundamental question: what did your character do before being drawn into this story? That answer defines not only what they can do, but how they see the world, solve problems, and relate to others. Two characters of the same species can feel very different if backgrounds differ.

## Available Backgrounds

### Martial Artist

The Martial Artist has devoted life to bodily perfection. Their training may come from combat schools, warrior clans, arenas, discipline rituals, or a life where the body was the only real survival tool. This background represents those who learned to overcome pain, fatigue, and adversity through repetition, resilience, and constant physical demand.

Major affinity: Physical Specializations.
Starting specializations: 2 Physical + 1 Mental.
Additionally, may replace any amount of starting competencies in species natural weapons with crafted weapons.

### Artisan

The Artisan represents someone who dedicated life to transforming matter, tools, and resources into useful, beautiful, or dangerous objects. Smiths, alchemists, tailors, carvers, builders, and similar trades fit this background. Their value is not only in making, but in observing the world through a technical, patient, precise lens.

Major affinity: Arts and Crafts.
Starting specializations: 2 Arts and Crafts + 1 Lore or 1 Social.

### Wanderer

The Wanderer is someone formed by the road. They had no fixed home, or if they once did, learned to live far from it. Their experiences took them across frontiers, ruins, foreign cities, and uncertain landscapes, where they developed ingenuity, intuition, and adaptation. This background embodies those who survive by reading signs, intentions, and opportunities where others see only threat or emptiness.

Major affinity: Mental Specializations.
Starting specializations: 2 Mental + 1 Physical.

### Custodian

The Custodian is heir to traditions, institutions, or duties tied to preserving knowledge. They may come from archives, temples, schools, libraries, guardian orders, or lineages tasked with protecting relics, writings, or memory. More than an impulsive explorer, the Custodian represents a character who understands the value of the old and feels moral weight in preserving or interpreting it correctly.

Major affinity: Lore.
Starting specializations: 2 Lore + 1 Social or 1 Mental.

### Noble

The Noble was born or raised in an environment shaped by privilege, education, hierarchy, and social ties, though not necessarily by current wealth or power. This background represents those trained to move through influence structures, negotiation, prestige, and duty. Their strength is often less in raw labor and more in projecting authority, reading interests, and moving wills.

Major affinity: Social.
Starting specializations: 1 Social + 2 from any other category, by choice.

## Important: Background and Synapsis

Starting specializations granted by background are not only a technical baseline. Since they begin at Level 1 / Rank 1, they immediately activate corresponding Synapsis and increase associated characteristics. Background therefore directly participates in final characteristic construction and is not limited to defining future progression.

## Specializations

Specializations are specific skills a character can develop through life. They reflect both theoretical knowledge and practical mastery, allowing characters to perform in many areas of competence. They are divided into major categories that cover different aspects of life and adventure: physical, mental, social, crafts, arts, and lore.

### Physical Skills

Physical skills require bodily dexterity, strength, agility, and control. They develop through training and intensive practice, allowing tasks that demand high conditioning and coordination. They are essential for survival in hostile environments, acrobatic maneuvers, and combat performance.

### Mental Skills

Mental skills include intellect, wisdom, and cunning. They are based on critical thinking, analysis, and application of theory in practical situations. Development involves study, observation, and intellectual practice, and is key to complex problem solving, pattern identification, and informed decisions.

### Social and Emotional Skills

Social and emotional skills focus on effective interaction and communication with others. They are essential for leading, persuading, deceiving, and negotiating, allowing characters to influence behavior and choices. Developing them requires communication practice, understanding social dynamics, and interpersonal management.

### Arts and Crafts

Crafts and arts are specializations combining theory and practical skill. Crafts include technical/manual abilities like smithing and carpentry, while arts include creative expression such as music, painting, and dance. They are developed through formal learning, intense practice, and creative experimentation.

### Lore

Lore represents deep specialized knowledge across disciplines such as alchemy, medicine, and astronomy. Development requires detailed study, investigation, and practical application of principles, granting advanced understanding and capacity for innovation.

### Physical Skills

### Jump

Jump specialization involves applying strength and technique to launch into the air from elevation or momentum in order to cross horizontal or vertical distances. Characters must calculate distance, momentum, and force carefully, adapting to environmental conditions. This specialization is key for overcoming physical obstacles in combat or exploration.

### Horizontal Jump

Perform a forward jump to cover horizontal distance. Requires calculating enough momentum to land safely at the desired point. Useful to cross pits, reach platforms, or escape danger.

Situations: jumping over pits, crossing cracks or abysses, reaching elevated combat positions.

### Vertical Jump

Jump upward to overcome vertical obstacles or reach high places. This requires high force control and precise technique to elevate the body.

Situations: climbing low walls, reaching ledges or tree branches, leaping to grab protrusions.

### Precision Jump

Ability to calculate and execute jumps with high precision, ensuring landing on narrow or dangerous surfaces where margin of error is minimal.

Situations: jumping between river stones, landing on narrow ledges, moving fast through tight spaces.

### Obstacle Jump

Perform a jump that includes evading or passing obstacles mid-path. Requires dynamic adjustment in the air and coordination to avoid collisions.

Situations: jumping through low branches, dodging airborne traps, crossing a battlefield.

### Power Jump

Execute a jump combining brute force and advanced technique to cover significant distance or greater-than-normal height.

Situations: jumping while in motion, making an extreme leap over a large abyss, jumping with heavy load or armor.

### Jump Types

| Jump | Jump Test |
|---|---|
| Horizontal | 2/meter |
| Vertical | 2/half meter |

### Penalties When Jumping

Modifiers to climbing can be additive depending on case.

| Situation | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Heavy armor | -(Piece Grade + 1) |
| Medium armor | -(Piece Grade) |
| Smooth terrain | -2 |
| Fatigue | -1 per Fatigue level |

- Related characteristic: Strength.
- Generates reaction: No.
- Test difficulty: Environment difficulty.
- Retries: According to situation.
- Type: Active.

## Select Starting Specializations

Starting specializations represent concrete fields where the character has developed practice, training, or knowledge before adventure start. Unlike characteristics, which express general aptitudes, specializations describe specific capabilities: actions, trades, lore fields, or practical skills exercised across life.

These specializations are grouped into broad categories that cover different domains of experience: Physical, Mental, Social and Emotional, Arts and Crafts, and Lore. Each category represents a different training mode.

During character creation you do not choose from all game specializations freely, but from those allowed by your background. Background determines how many starting specializations you gain and from which categories they may come. This ensures first competencies are coherent with prior life and training.

Each character gains three starting specializations when creating sheet, following restrictions from background. The same specialization cannot be chosen more than once. All selected options start at Level 1, equivalent to Rank 1.

Choosing starting specializations not only determines where the character stands out in early checks. It also defines a major part of identity. Two characters with the same background can feel very different if their starting specializations differ.

Additionally, each specialization is tied to an associated characteristic. This relation is not decorative: it expresses what part of the character is strengthened through practice of that skill. Since starting specializations begin at Rank 1, they activate Synapsis at creation and grant an increase to associated characteristics.

At this stage it is not necessary to detail every specific rule for each specialization. Full descriptions belong in the dedicated chapter. During creation, it is enough to choose three valid options by background, record Level 1 / Rank 1, record associated characteristic, and apply resulting Synapsis.

## Universal Starting Specialization: Vigor

In addition to the three starting specializations granted by background, every character begins with the Vigor specialization at Level 1 / Rank 1.

This reflects a basic reality of the world: surviving in this environment demands a minimum foundation of physical endurance, adaptation to effort, and tolerance for adverse conditions. Even before becoming an adventurer, every playable creature must have developed at least an elemental degree of vigor in order to subsist.

Since Vigor is a specialization associated with Tenacity, its initial rank also immediately activates the corresponding Synapsis.

Therefore, unless a specific rule states otherwise, every character gains at least +1 to Tenacity from their initial Vigor.

This increase does not replace bonuses granted by species. If the character's species also grants an increase to Tenacity, both values accumulate.

## Relationship Between Background and Starting Specializations

Your background not only defines which categories you can pick at start, but also where you will learn faster later. Category favored by background is your major affinity, and specializations within it require less progress to level up. Starting specializations are therefore part of a broader structure connecting prior history to later growth.

### Important

Complete specialization lists, with uses, common situations, penalties, difficulties, and specific rules, are in the Specializations chapter. During character creation you only need to choose starting specializations and record basic effects.

## Apply Synapsis

Synapsis represents how practice shapes the character. While species defines natural baseline, Synapsis expresses how training, repetition, and experience strengthen areas of body, mind, or behavior. In other words, Synapsis is the link between what the character has done and what they have become.

Each specialization is associated with a specific characteristic. Whenever a specialization reaches a new rank, the character gains a permanent increase in that associated characteristic. This increase reflects that repeated mastery does not only improve execution of a task, but transforms the character more deeply.

Synapsis applies both during creation and during progression. During creation, this occurs immediately after recording starting specializations. This includes both the specializations chosen through background and the universal starting specialization Vigor, which also begins at Level 1 / Rank 1.

Therefore, each recorded starting specialization grants +1 to its associated characteristic right away, including Vigor, which contributes +1 to Tenacity.

## General Synapsis Rule

Each time a specialization reaches a new rank, the character gains:

+1 to the characteristic associated with that specialization.

This increase is cumulative. If the same specialization reaches multiple ranks over time, it will keep strengthening the same characteristic at each advancement.

## How to Apply Synapsis During Creation

Once you have chosen your three starting specializations, apply Synapsis in this order:

1. Identify the associated characteristic for each specialization.
2. Verify current rank.
3. Add to the characteristic a value equal to that specialization's rank.

Since all starting specializations begin at Rank 1, each contributes +1 to its associated characteristic.

## Synapsis Accumulation

Synapsis from different specializations can accumulate on the same characteristic.

For example, if a character has several specializations linked to Cunning, each contributes to increasing that characteristic according to rank. This means one characteristic can grow through multiple paths, as long as related specializations were cultivated.

Thus, final characteristics depend not only on species, but also on the sum of areas where the character invested practice and experience.

## General Formula

After applying Synapsis, characteristic value is determined by:

Final Characteristic = Species Bonus + sum of ranks from all associated specializations

This formula clearly tracks origin of each value: one part from nature, one from development.

## Importance of Synapsis

Synapsis has a central role in character creation and progression. Thanks to it, specializations are not an isolated skill list but the direct source of characteristic growth. This makes development organic: the more a character practices, the more they change.

In this system, characteristics do not increase arbitrarily or through detached bonus packages. They increase because the character has lived, trained, failed, learned, and repeated. Synapsis is the mechanical expression of that process.

## Calculate Derived Attributes

Once species increases and Synapsis from starting specializations have been applied, you can calculate derived attributes.

Derived attributes are not primary traits. They are not chosen directly and do not begin with an independent base value. Instead, they represent more complex capacities emerging from the combination of multiple characteristics.

During character creation, two primary derived attributes are Preparation and Resilience.

### Preparation

Preparation reflects the character's ability to anticipate, react, and adapt to changing situations, especially under tension or combat. It depends not only on physical quickness, but also on Cunning to interpret events and Composure to act clearly under pressure.

Formula:

Preparation = (Agility + Cunning + Composure) / 3

### Resilience

Resilience represents the character's ability to withstand and recover from adverse alterations. It measures internal balance by combining physical endurance, judgment clarity, and emotional stability needed to resist effects that try to break or destabilize the character.

Formula:

Resilience = (Tenacity + Wisdom + Composure) / 3

## When They Are Calculated

Preparation and Resilience must be calculated after final characteristics are determined. Because both depend on multiple characteristics, they cannot be fixed before applying:

- species starting increases
- increases gained through Synapsis

This ensures derived attributes correctly reflect both nature and prior formation.

## How to Record Them

Once calculated, write both values on character sheet. From that point onward, those attributes may be used by other system rules when relevant.

In particular, Resilience is already part of resistance checks against alterations, so it should be recorded clearly from creation.

### Important

Derived attributes can change if their component characteristics increase later. Every time a relevant characteristic changes permanently, recalculate any derived attribute that depends on it.

This keeps derived attributes as a living expression of character growth rather than static values detached from evolution.

## Brief Example

Assume that after species and Synapsis, a character has:

Agility 2
Cunning 1
Composure 1
Tenacity 2
Wisdom 1

Then derived attributes are:

Preparation = (2 + 1 + 1) / 3 = 2 (rounded up)
Resilience = (2 + 1 + 1) / 3 = 2 (rounded up)

Both attributes therefore express a synthesis of several capabilities, not an isolated aptitude.

## Choose Personality Traits

Once characteristics, species, background, starting specializations, and derived attributes are defined, next step is determining how the character thinks, feels, and reacts to the world. This is represented through Personality Traits.

Personality traits do not describe what the character can do, but how they tend to behave, interpret events, and respond to situations. They are the most direct expression of temperament, emotional habits, and relation to others and self.

Beyond narrative value, personality traits also have mechanical function. In some situations, a trait can replace a specialization or characteristic check if the player convincingly explains how that trait influences the scene and the Narrator accepts that justification.

## The Five Factors

Each character chooses one personality trait for each of the five factors that structure psychological profile:

- Openness to Experience
- Conscientiousness
- Extraversion
- Agreeableness
- Neuroticism

These factors are not passing emotions, but deep tendencies. Together they provide a fuller image of how the character perceives world, acts with others, handles impulses, and responds to pressure.

## Trait Intensity

Each trait must be selected with high or low intensity. This is not simply having "more" or "less" of a quality: it defines the concrete direction in which that tendency manifests.

Within one factor, for example, a character may be deeply curious or prefer routine; very disciplined or impulsive; highly sociable or inclined to solitude. Chosen intensity helps define how the trait is lived in practice and avoids vague personality descriptions.

## What Each Factor Represents

### Openness to Experience

Reflects relation to novelty, uncertainty, abstraction, and imagination. It defines whether the character tends to explore, question, and experiment, or prefers known, stable, and proven paths.

### Conscientiousness

Represents discipline, order, consistency, and responsibility when facing duties and goals. It shows whether conduct is guided by organization, persistence, and self-control, or by dispersion, improvisation, and impulsiveness.

### Extraversion

Describes how the character projects outwardly. It helps define whether they actively seek interaction, initiative, and social intensity, or feel more comfortable with reserve, observation, and emotional distance.

### Agreeableness

Expresses disposition toward empathy, cooperation, trust, and consideration for others. It establishes whether the character approaches the world through collaboration and understanding, or through reserve, hardness, and distrust.

### Neuroticism

Reflects emotional stability and way of processing anxiety, pressure, sensitivity, and inner conflict. It helps determine whether the character tends to remain firm under tension or is more vulnerable to worry, reactivity, or emotional overflow.

## How to Choose Traits

For each of the five factors, choose:

- one personality trait
- its intensity: high or low

Chosen trait should help answer:

- How does the character face the unknown?
- How disciplined or impulsive are they?
- How do they behave around others?
- How willing are they to cooperate, trust, or yield?
- How do they handle pressure, fear, and instability?

The goal is not to choose "good traits," but coherent traits aligned with concept. An interesting character does not need to be balanced or admirable in every aspect; they need to feel alive, recognizable, and consistent.

## Traits and Fiction

Personality traits should connect with character history, species, background, and life experience. They are not arbitrary labels: they should explain why the character acts as they do, what wounds they carry, what habits they developed, and what aspects of character set them apart.

Two characters with the same background can feel very different if personality traits differ. Likewise, a well-chosen trait can reinforce or tension concept: an insecure noble does not feel like a dominant noble, and a curious custodian does not feel like a rigid one afraid of change.

## In-Game Use

When situation justifies it, a personality trait can replace a specialization or characteristic check. To do this, player must clearly describe how that trait influences behavior in current scene. If Narrator judges justification adequate, character may make a Personality Trait Check instead of normal check.

This makes personality not only an interpretive guide, but a legitimate avenue for action resolution through character identity.

### Important

Full list of suggested traits for each factor is in the Personality Traits section. During character creation, it is enough to choose one trait per factor and record whether intensity is high or low.

## Step Summary

At the end of this stage, your character should have:

- 1 trait for Openness to Experience
- 1 trait for Conscientiousness
- 1 trait for Extraversion
- 1 trait for Agreeableness
- 1 trait for Neuroticism
- intensity for each: high or low

## Record Additional Racial Attributes

Once species, background, starting specializations, Synapsis, and personality traits are chosen, the final character creation step is recording all additional racial attributes your species grants that are not yet on the sheet.

This step does not change core identity; it completes it. Its purpose is ensuring every species-derived trait is clearly recorded from the start so it can be used correctly during play. While characteristic bonuses were already applied when choosing species, this step records other elements defining bodily, sensory, and cultural nature.

These attributes include, depending on chosen species: size, height, weight, languages, speed, heritage, legacy, vigor, unarmed attacks, natural weapons, and any other special trait in species profile. If species has variants, this is also the moment to record which one belongs to your character and apply corresponding changes.

Recording these attributes matters because many are not purely descriptive. Some affect movement, perception, resistance to specific effects, or combat without crafted weapons. Others influence narrative interaction with the world, such as known languages, a specific heritage mark, or belonging to a certain species variant.

At this stage, review full species profile and ensure no trait remains unrecorded. If a racial capability grants situational bonuses, access to specific attacks, usage restrictions, or additional effects, all details should be recorded from the beginning.

## What You Must Record

At the end of this step, your sheet should include, in addition to already applied characteristic bonuses, every racial element relevant to your species. These may include:

- size, height, and weight
- starting languages
- movement speed
- heritage
- legacy
- vigor or equivalent starting values
- unarmed attacks or natural weapons
- variant, if species has one
- any additional special capability indicated in species description

### Important

Full description of these attributes is in the Species chapter. During character creation, you do not need to copy all cultural or narrative information into the sheet, but you should record every mechanical effect and functional trait that may enter play.

## Character Creation Closure

When you complete this step, your character will already have:

- a clear concept
- base characteristics modified by species and Synapsis
- a defined background
- three starting specializations
- derived attributes
- personality traits
- all required additional racial attributes

With this, character creation is complete and your character is ready to play.
