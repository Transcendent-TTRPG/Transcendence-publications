# Fiction Guide

This file governs long-form fiction in Transcendence: light novels, narrative supplements, in-world scenes, and sustained story passages.

Its role is not to define prose rhythm alone, but to define how fiction should carry setting truth without collapsing into lore-dump, system exposition, or generic dramatic writing.

---

## Core rule

Fiction reveals the world through pressure, action, perception, and consequence.

It does not explain the setting the way a sourcebook explains it.

If a paragraph exists mainly to teach the reader how the world works, it is probably no longer fiction. Reclassify it as `description`, `example`, or `rules`, or embed the information indirectly through dramatic context.

---

## What fiction is for

Use fiction to:

- reveal what life in the setting feels like from within
- show consequences before explaining causes
- stage conflict between belief, perception, and reality
- let readers infer world truth from constrained viewpoint
- attach emotional and sensory meaning to canon already defined elsewhere

Do not use fiction to:

- dump glossary information
- define rules directly
- settle unresolved canon questions implicitly
- bypass continuity constraints with “character voice” unless the text clearly marks it as limited or mistaken

---

## Fiction modes by scale

### Scene

Use when the text covers one continuous dramatic unit.

A scene should answer:

- who is under pressure
- what changed
- what the scene costs

### Sequence

Use when multiple scenes form one continuous push: pursuit, investigation beat, escape, confrontation buildup.

The sequence should have a single pressure line even if location or immediate action changes.

### Chapter

Use when a unit needs its own internal arc.

A chapter should not merely continue events. It should reframe them, escalate them, or reveal a new layer of cost.

---

## Viewpoint rules

- Prefer a constrained viewpoint.
- Do not describe facts the viewpoint cannot perceive, infer, remember, or believe.
- If narration states something beyond the viewpoint, that move must feel deliberate and rare.
- Do not slip between interior states of multiple characters inside one paragraph unless the text is explicitly using an omniscient mode.

For ordinary fiction, constrained close perspective is the default.

---

## Scene construction

Each scene should establish:

1. immediate situation
2. active source of pressure
3. what the viewpoint notices first
4. what shifts by the end of the scene

A scene does not need combat or spectacle, but it does need movement:

- a decision
- a discovery
- an escalation
- a loss
- a misreading
- a consequence

If none of these occur, the scene is probably static description rather than fiction.

---

## Tension rules

Fiction in Transcendence should carry controlled pressure.

That pressure may come from:

- danger
- uncertainty
- timing
- social friction
- bodily strain
- extranatural instability
- conflicting interpretations of what is happening

Not every scene must contain horror.
Not every scene must contain an anomaly.
But every scene should contain tension, asymmetry, or consequence.

---

## Dialogue rules

Dialogue exists to:

- reveal intent
- expose mismatch between characters
- distort or limit information
- change the pressure in the scene

Dialogue should not exist mainly to explain lore to the reader.

Avoid:

- characters stating things both already know for audience convenience
- long expository speeches unless the artificiality is intentional
- dialogue that explains cosmology more cleanly than the world itself would allow

Good dialogue usually leaves something unsaid.

---

## Lore revelation rules

Lore in fiction should usually be:

- partial
- situated
- biased
- costly

Preferred order:

1. effect
2. interpretation
3. partial explanation
4. later confirmation or contradiction

Do not reveal canon in fiction as if the narrator were a wiki unless the text is deliberately non-fictional.

---

## Limbo and horror in fiction

When fiction touches the Limbo or extranatural events:

- show the perceivable consequence first
- keep causality partially obscured unless the viewpoint has a reason to understand it
- use anomaly as pressure, not decoration
- preserve the distinction between theology, belief, and underlying world fact

Subtle manifestations are valid.
Not every Limbo-facing passage needs explosive contradiction.

---

## Description inside fiction

Description inside fiction must serve the scene.

It should do at least one of these:

- sharpen pressure
- direct attention
- mislead the viewpoint
- constrain action
- reveal consequence

If description only decorates the page, cut or compress it.

---

## Pacing rules

Long-form fiction should alternate among:

- compression
- contact
- consequence

Compression skips low-value transitions.
Contact places the reader inside active pressure.
Consequence shows what changed after contact.

Avoid flat pacing where every scene occupies the same emotional and structural weight.

---

## Character knowledge vs world truth

Characters may be wrong.
Beliefs may be inherited, doctrinal, distorted, or local.

That is allowed.

But the text must remain legible about whether a statement is:

- observable fact
- character belief
- theology
- rumor
- doctrine

If those layers blur accidentally, continuity breaks.

---

## Anti-patterns for fiction

Do not write:

- lore disguised as dialogue
- metaphoric overload
- generic “dark fantasy” mood language with no physical grounding
- horror explained too early
- scenes that repeat the same pressure without changing it
- conversations that only exist to transfer information cleanly

---

## Fiction drafting checklist

- [ ] The scene has a primary pressure line
- [ ] The viewpoint is clear
- [ ] The passage does not explain more than the viewpoint can justify
- [ ] Dialogue changes the scene rather than explaining the setting
- [ ] Description serves pressure or consequence
- [ ] Lore is revealed through situated perception, belief, or cost
- [ ] The scene ends with change, not mere stopping

---

## Product guidance

### Light novel

- default modes: `narrative`, `description`, `flavor`
- lore is revealed gradually and indirectly
- rules language should almost never appear directly

### Narrative supplement

- may alternate between `description` and `narrative`
- can tolerate more explicit context than a novel
- should still avoid sourcebook exposition inside scenes

### Corebook flavor scene

- keep short
- prioritize atmosphere and implication
- do not let the insert carry rules-critical information
