This file is the rhythm reference for all Transcendence prose. Samples here are treated as the gold standard. When rewriting a passage, find a sample with similar purpose and use it as a template for cadence and vocabulary — not content.

---

## How to use this file

1. Identify the purpose of the passage you are writing (rules explanation, scene-setting, example, etc.).
2. Find a sample below with the same purpose.
3. Read the sample aloud to internalize the rhythm.
4. Write your passage. Match the sentence length pattern. Match the level of technical density. Do not copy phrases.

## Approved samples — ES

### Rules explanation

La Preparación determina qué tan pronto una criatura entra en el flujo del combate. No representa velocidad pura, sino la capacidad de actuar con decisión cuando la violencia todavía no ha fijado su ritmo. Al inicio del enfrentamiento, cada participante ocupa una posición en el track según su valor de Preparación y los modificadores de la situación. Quien se encuentre más adelantado resuelve primero. A partir de ese punto, cada acción desplaza su marcador según el costo indicado. El combate no avanza por rondas cerradas, sino por la acumulación de elecciones: actuar antes exige pagar antes, y pagar antes significa ceder espacio en el tiempo del conflicto.

El Desgaste representa el agotamiento acumulado que sufre una criatura al forzar acciones dentro del combate. No es un recurso que se gaste de forma aislada, sino una presión creciente sobre el cuerpo y la capacidad de respuesta. Cada acción relevante incrementa este valor según su costo. A medida que el Desgaste aumenta, comienzan a aplicarse penalizadores a tiradas clave, reflejando la pérdida de precisión, fuerza o control. Este deterioro no es inmediato, pero tampoco es opcional: ignorarlo permite actuar más en el corto plazo, pero compromete la viabilidad del personaje conforme el conflicto se prolonga.

Una acción reactiva permite intervenir fuera del momento propio en el flujo del combate, pero siempre a un costo superior. Estas acciones representan decisiones forzadas: responder en desventaja, interrumpir un ritmo ya establecido o exponerse para alterar el resultado inmediato. Al ejecutarlas, el marcador avanza como cualquier otra acción, pero el incremento de Desgaste es mayor. Esto implica que reaccionar no solo altera el orden, sino también el estado del personaje. No existe reacción sin consecuencia; cada intervención adelantada debe pagarse en estabilidad futura.

### Scene or narrative

El bosque no había muerto, pero el suelo ya no respondía como antes. Cada paso cedía apenas, con una resistencia blanda que obligaba a ajustar el equilibrio, como si la tierra no terminara de sostener el peso. El sonido llegaba después. No de inmediato, sino con un leve retraso, lo suficiente para romper el ritmo de la marcha. No había movimiento entre los árboles. Solo esa pequeña desincronización, constante, imposible de ignorar una vez que se percibía.

El aire no cambiaba, pero la respiración sí. Cada inhalación parecía requerir un esfuerzo adicional, como si algo en el entorno ofreciera una resistencia leve pero constante. No era falta de oxígeno, sino una incomodidad difícil de ubicar, suficiente para alterar el ritmo natural del cuerpo. Tras unos minutos, el grupo comenzó a ajustar su paso sin decirlo. No más lento. Más contenido. Como si avanzar demasiado rápido fuera una forma de llamar la atención.

Las marcas en los árboles no estaban donde deberían. Algunas parecían recientes, otras demasiado antiguas para mantenerse visibles, pero ninguna coincidía con la dirección en la que avanzaban. Al intentar seguirlas, el recorrido se volvía inconsistente: lo que antes indicaba una ruta clara terminaba desviándose sin explicación. No era que el camino desapareciera. Era que dejaba de comportarse como un camino.

## Approved samples — EN

### Rules explanation

Preparation determines how early a creature enters the rhythm of combat. It is not raw speed, but the ability to impose intent before violence settles into pattern. At the start of an encounter, each participant is placed on the track according to their Preparation value and the relevant situational modifiers. The leftmost position acts first. From that moment on, every action advances that marker by its listed cost. Combat does not proceed in sealed rounds, but through accumulated consequences: acting sooner requires paying sooner, and paying sooner means surrendering ground in the timeline of the fight.

Attrition represents the accumulated strain a creature experiences when forcing actions within combat. It is not a resource spent in isolation, but a growing pressure on the body and its ability to respond. Each relevant action increases this value according to its cost. As Attrition rises, penalties begin to apply to key rolls, reflecting a loss of precision, strength, or control. This deterioration is neither immediate nor avoidable: ignoring it allows for short-term activity, but steadily undermines the character’s viability as the conflict continues.

A reactive action allows a creature to intervene outside of its own position in the combat flow, but always at an increased cost. These actions represent forced decisions: responding at a disadvantage, interrupting an established rhythm, or exposing oneself to alter an immediate outcome. When executed, the marker advances as with any other action, but the Attrition gained is higher. This means reacting does not only change order—it alters state. There is no reaction without consequence; every early intervention must be paid for in future stability.

### Scene or narrative

The forest had not died, but the ground no longer responded as it should. Each step gave slightly underfoot, a soft resistance that forced a subtle shift in balance, as though the earth could not fully bear the weight. The sound came after. Not immediately, but with a slight delay—enough to break the rhythm of movement. Nothing stirred between the trees. Only that small desynchronization, constant, impossible to ignore once noticed.

The air did not change, but breathing did. Each inhale required a subtle adjustment, as though something in the environment resisted without fully blocking it. It was not a lack of oxygen, but a discomfort difficult to locate, enough to disrupt the body’s natural rhythm. After a few minutes, the group adjusted their pace without speaking. Not slower. More controlled. As if moving too quickly might draw attention.

The markings on the trees were not where they should be. Some appeared recent, others too old to remain visible, yet none aligned with the direction of travel. Attempting to follow them led to inconsistency: what seemed like a clear path would gradually diverge without explanation. It was not that the trail vanished. It simply stopped behaving like one.

## Counter-examples — what NOT to write

The forest was dark and mysterious, filled with an unsettling presence that made every step feel dangerous. The air was thick with tension, and it seemed like something was watching them from the shadows. Every movement felt wrong, and the deeper they went, the more they felt like they did not belong in that place.

The forest breathed with a suffocating, decaying pulse, its roots whispering secrets of forgotten tides while the air clung to the skin like a damp, sentient shroud. Each step echoed through unseen layers of reality, bending perception into something fragile and unreliable, as if the very concept of movement was unraveling under the weight of an ancient, watching consciousness.

The forest feels unnatural because the ley line that once sustained it has been disrupted, causing the environment to behave inconsistently. This results in delayed sound feedback and unstable terrain, which are symptoms of the area attempting to stabilize itself after losing its connection to the natural flow of energy.

## Observations extracted from samples

Patterns derived from the approved samples above. Use these as calibration when writing new passages.

### Sentence length pattern

- Rules text: most sentences are 15–30 words. Closing sentences are consistently shorter (5–15 words) and carry the most weight.
- Narrative: opening sentence establishes the base state or anomaly (20–30 words); rhythm sentences within the paragraph shorten as the paragraph develops; the final sentence is the shortest and most declarative.
- The weight of a paragraph is front-loaded (opening) or closing-loaded (resolution). Middle sentences carry the mechanism.

### Paragraph density

- Rules: 4–6 sentences. One concept per paragraph. Do not combine two rules into the same paragraph.
- Narrative: 4–6 sentences. One anomaly per paragraph. Each paragraph ends when the anomaly has been fully registered — not when it has been explained.

### Vocabulary register

- Rules text: zero similes. Verbs carry description ("advances", "compresses", "applies"). No emotional language.
- Narrative: maximum one simile per paragraph, always grounded in the physical ("como si la tierra no terminara de sostener el peso"). Never stacked. Never abstract.
- Adjectives are used sparingly. When used, they describe a measurable property ("blanda", "leve", "constante"), not a subjective quality ("unsettling", "dark", "strange").

### How mechanical concepts are introduced

- Always by contrast: "No es X, sino Y" / "It is not X, but Y."
- The contrast names what the concept is NOT before defining what it IS.
- Then: consequence chain, one step at a time. Each sentence advances one step.
- Final sentence: the trade-off or constraint, stated briefly without hedging.

### How anomalies are structured in narrative

1. State the base fact: what has not changed or what is expected.
2. Introduce the deviation: what behaves differently, described physically.
3. Behavioral consequence: what characters do in response — without explanation of why.
4. Do not state the cause. Do not resolve the tension. Leave it present.

### Counter-example failure modes

- **Hollow/generic:** vague adjectives ("dark", "mysterious", "unsettling"), no specific sensory detail, emotional labeling without grounding ("felt wrong").
- **Overwrought:** stacked metaphors, multiple sensory channels simultaneously, abstract personification ("sentient shroud", "ancient consciousness").
- **Meta-explanatory:** describes the cause of the phenomenon instead of the phenomenon itself ("because the ley line has been disrupted"). This violates the Narrative vs Rules boundary — narrative shows, it does not explain.
